Trajectory Pathlet Dictionary Construction using Deep Reinforcement Learning

Date Completed: January 16, 2023





The recent and modern advancements for geo-based tracking technologies and location-based smart devices has led to vast amounts of mobility data coming from various moving objects, such as in vehicles like cars or taxis, as well as in human mobility such as pedestrians. These spatiotemporal-based trajectory data motivates various analytic tasks such as trajectory prediction, clustering, and anomaly detection. Of particular interest is the construction of small sets of building blocks that can represent a wide range of trajectories known as a trajectory pathlet dictionary or a pathlet dictionary (PD) for short. The pioneering work that coined the term pathlets, or subpaths of the road network, constructed PDs using an integer programming optimization formulation and then reduced it to a problem that is solvable using dynamic programming. Other works constructed pathlet dictionaries by framing the problem of interest as a subtrajectory clustering problem. The downsides of these methods are that these methods construct dictionaries using traditional, non-learning based methods (i.e., without the use of deep learning), and that these dictionaries are constructed using a top-down heuristic. What that means is that all (overlapping) pathlets of sizes and configurations are first initialized, and then the candidate size is reduced by taking only the top-most (popular) ones; as a consequence, such methods require a massive amount of memory space to initialize such pathlets.

We exploit on these weaknesses by proposing a bottom-up method that first initializes unit-length (edge-disjoint) pathlets, and then merging together adjacent, neighboring ones that altogether aim to satisfy a certain objective to reduce that candidate dictionary size. This objective function is based on utility theory, i.e., the utility of a pathlet on the road network. Deciding which pathlets to merge together is a tricky task, and as such we relied on two proposed novel trajectory metrics (the trajectory representability and loss) to formulate the aformentioned objective function. This allows us to more comprehensively evaluate the generated dictionaries. Moreover, a deep (reinforcement) learning method that is based on a Deep Q Network (DQN) policy has been utilized for learning the best optimal sequence of actions (merge/unmerge of pathlets) to construct the said dictionary. In particular, its reward function is shaped around the utility which we aim to maximize. As a result, the problem of constructing pathlet dictionaries has been reduced to a utility maximization problem using deep reinforcement learning. We observed in our experiments that our proposed DRL method can outperform the null singleton model (the model that does not do any kind of pathlet merging), as well as state-of-the-art traditional baselines; these non-learning based models do not use deep learning methods and as a result perform poorly in comparison to our method that is based on reinforcement learning. This observation highlights the essence of deep learning. Our model can construct a dictionary that is up to 66% smaller than the dictionary of the baselines. Ablation studies have also shown that our proposed method can achieve convergence as early as 15 iterations of the model training compared to its ablation versions. Partial trajectory reconstruction experiments show that our proposed model can reconstruct up to 85% of the evaluation trajectories provided only 50% of the constructed pathlet dictionary (and reconstruct 100% of the evaluation trajectory data provided the whole PD). This is contrast to the random model that reconstructs only 50% of the evaluation trajectories and that is on the assumption that the entirety of the dictionary is used. This proves the effectivity of our proposed method.

Epidemic Spreading with Trajectory Data Mining

Date Completed: November 1, 2022





Conventional epidemic models include the tradtional SIR compartmental model, but however these traditional methods lack the granularity needed for analyzing individual mobility and behaviors during an epidemic that would aid in understanding the relationship between individual decisions and epidemic spreading. In contrast to macroscopic models, microscopic epidemic models focus on individuals and their properties, such as their activity level, mobility behaviors, and impact of mobility behavior changes. Our microscopic spatiotemporal epidemic model allows to: (i) assess the risk of infection of an individual based on mobility patterns; (ii) assess the risk of infection associated with specific geographic areas and points-of-interest (POIs); (iii) assess the risk of infection of a trip in an urban environment; (iv) provide trip recommendation for mitigating the risk of infection; and (v) assess targeted intervention strategies that aim to control the epidemic spreading. Our work provides an evidence-based data-driven model to inform individuals about the infection risks associated with their mobility behavior during a pandemic, providing at the same time safer alternatives. It can also inform public policy about the effectiveness of targeted intervention strategies that aim to contain or mitigate the epidemic spread compared to horizontal measures.

In addition, we have designed and developed STRIPE (Safe Trips during Epidemics), a mobility-based recommendation system that can provide safer trip recommendations to individuals. The recommendation model considers the risk of infection of alternative trips between an origin and destination. It also considers the risk of infection of specific points of interests (POIs) that occur at the microscale. In this paper, we present a high-level architecture of the system, its main features and system use cases. The broader impact of our research is that by helping individuals making informed decisions, we promote more responsible behaviors in the community as a whole that could effectively alleviate the impact of the epidemic.

Machine Learning Algorithms for Laser-induced Breakdown Spectroscopy Datasets

Date Completed: April 15, 2022


The exploration and analyses of chemical components in (extra-)terrestrial geological materials (such as asteroids and meteorites) are insightful in modern research. Laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy (LIBS) is a popular method for analyzing the chemical attributes of geologic samples - which scientists use to study and understand planetary bodies and their complex histories. In the literature, several machine learning models that produce high-accuracy predictions have been proposed. In our work, we compared the performances of such models in predicting elemental abundances on a certain spectroscopic dataset. Models included partial least squares (PLS), extreme gradient boost machines (XGB), neural networks, and linear models. In our results, we showed how PLS and XGB are superior in terms of high predictive power, their ability to generalize, and their reasonably efficient runtimes. In addition, we proposed Ensemble models that aggregate predictions of top-tier models and observed that they can be desirable (can reduce errors of top models PLS and XGB by up to 25.6% and 53.9%). We intend to gain better understanding of how these models perform in predicting elemental compositions on specific spectrum (LIBS) datasets.

Forest Fire Analytics

Date Completed: December 6, 2020




Forest fires have been common environmental issues that have impacted millions of lives and natural resources. Thus, an in-depth study of the behavior of wildfires is critical in devising preventive strategies to control its rapid spread. However, despite a significant amount of work that has been done in the literature, developing an effective fire spread model is still a challenge.

Our goal is to design a model of forest fire propagation and be able to simulate the behavior of cascading fires in the forest. More specifically, we propose INCINERATE (INformation Cascades In NEtworkX Representing A Forest of Trees Engulfed by flames), a fire simulator in forest network graphs. A Linear Threshold model, together with various mathematical models from literature, were employed in this simulation that “determines” the behavior of fire propagation. We then propose fire prevention strategies in order to mitigate the damage of forest fires. In particular, we propose the FIGHTER (Forest fire Inhibition via a Girvan-Newman Heuristic Through a neighborhood-based Edge-Removal Approach) algorithm, a neighborhood-based edge-removal scheme utilizing the Girvan-Newman heuristic. It has been found that our edge-removal algorithm in forest networks can reduce the impact of wildfires by as much as 85% than if there were no preventive measures.

This paper discusses in-detail the models, algorithms and results of our experiments, as well as suggested further research directions.
