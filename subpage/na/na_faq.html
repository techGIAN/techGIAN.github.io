What and Why

I recently have gotten some interest in numerical methods (or analysis, interchangeably). It is a field in Applied Math where modern approximation techniques are covered. It also explains how methods work, usually in an algorithmic form of way. At what certain conditions are they expected to work and what relevant theorems and remarks can be concluded.

Let's take an example.

We were taught in high school that the derivative of a function $f(x)$ at some point $x=x_0$ is simply $f'(x_0)$, which can easily be obtained by finding the derivative of the function $f(x)$ and then evaluating at point $x_0$. This would be easy for the easier and simplier functions such as say $f(x) = x^2$. We get $f'(x) = 2x$. And if say our $x_0 = 1$, then $f'(x_0) = 2(1) = 2$. (If you are not too familiar with the concepts and ideas of Calculus and derivatives, I highly recommend going over KhanAcademy's YouTube video by clicking here.)

One problem one may face here is that $f(x)$ may be some complicated (or monstrous even) function that it may be infeasbile to find its derivative. Luckily, we may be able to define $f'(x_0)$ through the use of a numerical formula, that is to say an approximation to $f'(x_0)$ without having to calculate its derivative. Recall the limit definition of a derivative evaluated at $x=x_0$: $$f'(x_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$$ where $h$ is the step size (usually for smaller values of $h$ tending to approach $h = 0$). Then we can approximate $f'(x_0)$ simply as: $$f'(x_0) \approx \dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$$ where $h$ is the step size and we would like to use small values of $h$, such as $h=0.1,0.01,...$.

Of course, since Numerical Analysis is all about approximations, nothing gives exact answers. There is always some error $\epsilon$ that exists, and this is why a part of the study of Numerical Analysis also deals with the study of error.

What's nice about it is that since nothing in this world is exact (for instance human measurement can never be exact due to human errors or inaccurate measuring devices, so a length of 5 meters is not exactly 5 meters but could be a little more or less than that), then Numerical Analysis pretty much has an infinitely many practical uses and applications after all.
